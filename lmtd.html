Answer

The log mean temperature difference (LMDT) is the logrithmic average of the temperature differences between the hot and cold streams on each side of a heat exchanger. For a counter-current heat exchanger (most common) the temperature differences are: \[ \Delta_A = T_{hot in} - T_{cold out} \] \[ \Delta_B = T_{hot out} - T_{cold in} \] \[ LMDT = \frac{\Delta_A - \Delta_B}{\log{ \left( \Delta_A - \Delta_B \right)}} \]

A cooling heat exchanger brings juice at 121°C down to 75°C by using cold propylene glycol. The propylene glycol enters the heat exchanger at 25°C and exits at 50°C. What is the log mean temperature difference?

Find ΔA: \[ \Delta_A = T_{hot in} - T_{cold out} \] \[ \Delta_A = 121 - 50 = 71 \] Find ΔB: \[ \Delta_B = T_{hot out} - T_{cold in} \] \[ \Delta_B = 75 - 25 = 50\] Find LMTD: \[ LMDT = \frac{\Delta_A - \Delta_B}{\log{ \left( \Delta_A - \Delta_B \right)}} \] \[ LMDT = \frac{71 - 50}{\log{ \left( 71 - 51 \right)}} \] \[ LMDT = 59.89 \text{°C} \]

Heat Exchangers

The log mean temperature difference is useful for calculating how much heat is transfered in a heat exchanger. \[ Q = U \times A \times LMTD \] Q is the heat duty for the heat exchanger, U is the overall heat transfer coefficient for the heat exchanger, and A is the heat exchange surface area.
